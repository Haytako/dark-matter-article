    <h3>Given (all the world's knowledge):</h3>
    <ol>
      <li><strong>Einstein's General Relativity:</strong> Gravity is the curvature of spacetime.</li>
      <li><strong>Antimatter (QED/Feynman):</strong> Mathematically, an antiparticle is an ordinary particle moving backward in time. A positron is an electron flying from the future into the past.</li>
      <li><strong>The Baryon Asymmetry Problem:</strong> We are searching for the "excess" matter that arose from a tiny symmetry violation in the early Universe.</li>
      <li><strong>Properties of Dark Matter:</strong> It has mass (it gravitates), but it does not interact with light or ordinary matter.</li>
    </ol>

    <div class="hypothesis-box">
      <p>What if dark matter is not a substance in space, but a deformation of spacetime left behind after time "forgot" the antiparticles?</p>
    </div>

    <p>Picture the moment right after the Big Bang. Particles and antiparticles are born and annihilate. But in certain acts of annihilation, due to quantum fluctuations and CP violation, the time loop does not fully close. The particle moved forward (became our matter). The antiparticle was supposed to go backward in time and erase the "debt." But due to the asymmetry, the antiparticle's timeline was severed, leaving the circuit unclosed.</p>

    <p>What remains after such an unclosed time loop? A "ripple" on the fabric of spacetime. A microscopic wormhole with no entrance&thinsp;&mdash;&thinsp;simply a knot of curvature.</p>

    <h4>Verification:</h4>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Does it not emit light?</strong> &mdash; No. It is not a particle; it is a topological defect.</li>
      <li><strong>Does it attract gravitationally?</strong> &mdash; Yes. According to GR, any curvature of space creates a gravitational effect.</li>
      <li><strong>Does this explain where the antimatter went?</strong> &mdash; Yes. It transitioned into the structure of space itself.</li>
    </ul>
